Mission

Terraform

Terraforming is a hypothetical process involving the deliberate modification of a planet's or natural satellite's atmosphere, temperature, topography, or ecology to make it hospitable to Earth-based life.

Sending astronauts to Mars is one of NASA's primary goals for the 21st century. In preparation for this long-duration spaceflight, NASA has been studying the effects that such a journey could have on astronaut health. Using various research platforms, NASA is continually learning more about the hazards of deep space exploration and how to mitigate those effects. The International Space Station and the upcoming Artemis Moon missions might come to mind immediately, but NASA also conducts analog missions here on Earth that simulate what it would be like to live in other parts of the solar system. One example is NASA Extreme Environment Mission Operations (NEEMO), where astronauts, engineers, and scientists study how to live and work in the depths of the Atlantic Ocean. NASA's Human Research Program (HRP) has categorized the hazards astronauts face into five classifications, allowing for an organized effort to understand and overcome the obstacles standing in the way of a deep space mission. It is important to understand that these risks are not isolated; they are interrelated, and one hazard can exacerbate the effects of another on the human body. The five research areas create the acronym RIDGE.
